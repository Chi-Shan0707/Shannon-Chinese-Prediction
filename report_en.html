A tiny Shannon experiment on Chinese

I Tried Shannon’s Guessing Game on Chinese Texts

I fed a tiny language model everything from Wikipedia to KFC Thursday memes, just to see where Chinese starts becoming guessable.

Qwen3-0.6B Base · 11 Chinese text categories · 59,496 prediction steps

When the model saw “因为疯狂”, it wanted to say “星期四” immediately. Rank 1. When it saw “肯德基疯狂”, same story: “星期四” was still right there. But when the setup became “V我50吃一顿...”, the word “肯德基” disappeared from the top guesses.

That tiny failure is what made the project interesting. The model knew the phrase, but not always the joke.

One thing I love about Shannon’s entropy experiment is how physical it feels. Someone just sits there guessing the next letter, and the text gives up its structure one mistake at a time. I wanted to try the same idea on Chinese, and immediately ran into the obvious problem: what exactly is “the next symbol”?

Chinese gives you characters, words, idioms, names, memes, and then tokenizers come in and slice everything into their own strange pieces. So I let a small local model be the guesser.

The Trick Was Not Asking for One Number

I stopped trying to get “the entropy of Chinese” and started asking a smaller question: where does the model suddenly know what comes next?

This is not a claim about the true entropy of Chinese. The model is just my measuring stick. But the measuring stick is still useful: at every position in a text, it shows how concentrated or scattered its expectations are. If the next phrase is obvious, probability collapses onto a few candidates. If the text gets dialectal, literary, weird, or shaped like a punchline, the model starts spreading its bets.

That made the project feel less like a benchmark and more like poking at texture. I was not really asking whether the model was good. I was asking what kind of Chinese made it pause.

Genre shows up in the curve. Wikipedia and news look smooth and predictable. Dialect fiction does not. That sounds obvious, but it is satisfying to watch the difference appear as a curve rather than as a vague literary impression.
Formula helps, but not the way I expected. Romance of the Three Kingdoms is easy to hit in the top-1000 candidates, but not the lowest-entropy text. A formula can make the right answer available without making the model completely certain.
The meme test split cleanly in two. “疯狂 → 星期四” is nearly automatic. “吃 → 肯德基” is not. The first is a fixed collocation; the second asks the model to recognize the social script of the meme.
Context helps in a very Shannon-looking way. More context helps, but the first few dozen characters do most of the work. After that, the returns flatten. That is the part that feels closest to Shannon.

This became the fun part for me. Instead of just saying “these texts feel different,” I could point to the exact places where the model got nervous: where it became confident, where it hesitated, and what kind of language caused the hesitation.

How I Made the Model Guess

I let the model read each text one tiny step at a time. At each step I asked two things: is the real continuation somewhere among its guesses, and how spread-out are those guesses?

Technical detail: the script checks the model’s top-1000 next-token candidates. If one candidate is a prefix of the real continuation, it counts as a hit and the script advances by that token length. If none matches, it records a miss and moves forward by one character.

The main number I track is just the entropy of the model’s next-token distribution:

\[H(q)=-\sum_t q(t)\log q(t).\]

Because the code uses natural logarithms, the unit is nats per prediction step. This detail matters. These values are not directly comparable to character-level results such as bits/character. But inside this little setup, they are good enough to compare one text against another.

What I Fed the Model

I used 11 categories, ranging from Wikipedia and news to classical fiction, wuxia, modern literature, dialect fiction, internet twists, and KFC Thursday memes. I was not trying to build a balanced corpus. I wanted to throw very different kinds of Chinese into the same machine and see which differences still showed up.

Wikipedia Glides; Dialect Fiction Snags

This is roughly what I expected. Wikipedia and news are built from conventional expository patterns: definitions, named entities, time-place relations, topic labels, and reporting formulas. Dialect fiction makes the model work harder. There is more local voice, more spoken rhythm, and more phrasing that probably did not appear a million times in the model’s training data.

I like this result because it makes a fuzzy reading feeling show up as a curve. “方言味儿” and “口语感” are not just stylistic impressions here; they become a higher-entropy prediction environment.

The Three Kingdoms Surprise

Romance of the Three Kingdoms has the highest top-1000 match rate here: 95.0%. But it does not have the lowest mean entropy. This was my favorite small surprise.

My guess is this: formulaic writing gets the right continuation into the model’s neighborhood, but the model can still remain uncertain among several plausible continuations. Classical narrative formulas such as “却说”, “话说”, “玄德曰”, battle descriptions, titles, and repeated narrative patterns make the text guessable in a broad sense. But the probability mass is not necessarily concentrated on one token as strongly as in Wikipedia.

So “the model can find it somewhere” is not the same as “the model is sure.”

The Model Knows “Crazy Thursday”—But Not Always the Joke

If you have spent any time on Chinese internet, you know the move: a normal or dramatic setup suddenly turns into “today is Crazy Thursday, V me 50.” So I tried poking the punchline directly.

The model clearly has “疯狂星期四” stored as a chunk. But “eat → KFC” is much harder, because “eat” can be followed by countless food nouns. Getting to KFC requires more than grammar; it requires recognizing the bit.

That is why the meme test ended up being more useful than I expected. It splits predictability into two things: phrases the model has memorized, and jokes whose setup it actually has to recognize.

More Context Helps—Until It Mostly Doesn’t

After genre and memes, I wanted to ask the more Shannon-like question: how much previous text actually helps? For four longer texts, I extended the context window from \(N=5\) up to about \(N=200\) and fit a simple decay curve:

\[H(N)=H_\infty+aN^{-b}.\]

I would not over-interpret the exact fitted parameters. The shape matters more than the fitted numbers: context helps a lot early, then helps less and less. The first few dozen characters buy you a lot; the next hundred buy less. That curve feels very Shannon to me.

What This Is Not Measuring

Here is the caveat I do not want to bury. It would be tempting to call this “the entropy of Chinese.” I would not.

  • The unit is nats/step, not bits/character.
  • The predictor is one small language model, not the true distribution of Chinese.
  • The game uses token matching, while classic entropy estimates are usually character-level.

If I wanted to do this properly at the character level, I would need to fold token probabilities back into character probabilities:

\[P(c\mid C)=\sum_{t:\operatorname{prefix}(t)=c} P(t\mid C).\]

That would make the comparison with older bits-per-character work much less awkward. This version is the flashlight walk: look for the interesting shapes first, then decide whether it is worth building the sharper instrument.

What I Still Find Interesting

The reason I kept caring about this was that the questions felt linguistic, not just model-y:

  • Which kinds of Chinese make the model hesitate?
  • Does formulaic writing make the model confident, or just put the right answer somewhere nearby?
  • Does the model know the phrase, or does it know the joke?
  • How much context do you need before more text stops helping much?

Those questions feel more interesting to me than one final entropy number. That is why I am putting this here, as a note rather than a paper: it is a small computational note about texture, not an attempt to solve Chinese information theory.

If I Had to Keep One Sentence

It would be:

A tiny model cannot tell me the entropy of Chinese, but it can show me where different Chinese texts make prediction easy, weird, or fragile.

If I come back to this, I want to add character-level probabilities, confidence intervals, more models, and more spoken/web-native text. Even in this rough form, it already shows the thing I was hoping to see: different Chinese texts are predictable in different ways, and even a tiny local model can help point to where those differences are hiding.
